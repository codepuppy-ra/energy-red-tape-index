## 1. INTRODUCTION (1.5 pages)

### 1.1 Problem Statement

Canada has experienced a succession of delayed, rejected, and cancelled major energy infrastructure projects over the past decade:

- **Energy East** ($15.7B crude pipeline, cancelled 2017)
- **Northern Gateway** ($7.9B crude pipeline, cancelled 2016)
- **Keystone XL** (cross-border, delayed/cancelled)
- **Pacific NorthWest LNG** ($36B LNG export, cancelled 2017)
- **Teck Frontier** ($20.6B oil sands mine, withdrawn 2020)

These projects represent over $88 billion in proposed capital expenditure, now either abandoned or indefinitely delayed. While commodity price fluctuations and project economics play roles, industry leaders and government documents point to regulatory and legal uncertainty as material contributors to project abandonment.

## 2. LITERATURE REVIEW (2–2.5 pages)

### 2.1 Regulatory Uncertainty and Investment

Start with **Baker, Bloom & Davis (2016)**: Economic Policy Uncertainty (EPU) is distinct from general macroeconomic volatility and has measurable effects on business investment, employment, and output.

- VAR evidence shows EPU shocks reduce investment and production
- Identification comes from policy sources (not economic fundamentals)

**Application to Canada:** The Impact Assessment Act (Bill C-69, 2019) and constitutional uncertainty around federal energy jurisdiction (2019–2023) represent policy-driven EPU shocks to Canadian energy sector.

### 2.2 Regulatory Barriers in Canada

**C.D. Howe Institute (2025):** Canada's Product Market Regulation (PMR) score is 1.43 (2018), vs. top-performer average of 0.8. Restrictive upstream regulations in energy reduce investment, innovation, and competition.

**OECD Economic Surveys (2025):** Canada faces structural productivity and regulatory barriers. Recommends reducing regulatory barriers in internal markets to boost investment.

**Fraser Institute (2023):** Executive survey of 165 upstream oil/gas leaders ranks Canada lower than most US states on regulatory certainty. Alberta ranks 3rd, BC ranks last among Canadian provinces.

### 2.3 Approval Timelines and Supply Response

**Gabriel & Kunga (2022):** Permitting delay and approval uncertainty independently constrain new development supply. Using LA housing data, they find 37% of average project completion time consumed by regulatory waiting. A 25% reduction in permitting length would increase completions by 20.9%.

**Application:** Similar approval-lag logic applies to energy projects. Each year of review delay raises project risk, costs, and abandonment probability.

### 2.4 Regulatory Costs and Long-Run Growth

**Coffey, McLaughlin & Peretto (2020):** Regulatory accumulation's cost is not compliance spending but distortion of investment choices. Using Schumpeterian endogenous growth model, they find regulatory accumulation since 1980 dampened GDP growth by ~0.8% per annum, leaving 2012 US economy ~$4 trillion smaller.

**Application:** Foregone energy investment in Canada reduces capital formation, which dampens long-run GDP. The loss is not just "delayed" but partially permanent if it crowds out future investment.

### 2.5 The Gap

Existing literature establishes:
- ✓ Regulatory uncertainty affects investment (Baker, Bloom, Davis)
- ✓ Canada has higher regulatory burden than peers (OECD, C.D. Howe)
- ✓ Executives cite regulatory delays as barriers (Fraser, CEO surveys)

**Missing:** Project-level quantification of **foregone GDP** and attribution of the **regulatory share**.

This paper provides that missing empirical link.

## 3. INSTITUTIONAL BACKGROUND (1.5 pages)

### 3.1 Evolution of Canadian Energy Regulation

#### Pre-2012: National Energy Board Regime

- NEB established 1959; reviewed major pipelines and export licensing
- Streamlined approval process (typically 2–3 years)
- Economic case heard; engineering reviewed; some environmental assessment

#### 2012–2019: Strengthened Environmental Review

- 2012: Canadian Environmental Assessment Act (CEAA 2012) expanded scope
- 2013–2017: NEB reviews become longer; scope creep common
- **2017 Energy East decision:** NEB expanded scope to include upstream/downstream emissions mid-review; TransCanada withdrew after 5 years of work
- Policy signal: regulatory goalposts can shift mid-process

#### 2019–2025: Impact Assessment Act and Constitutional Uncertainty

- **June 2019:** Impact Assessment Act (Bill C-69) received royal assent
- Expanded federal jurisdiction; added climate considerations; added consultation requirements
- Federal/provincial jurisdictional ambiguity (2019–2023): multiple legal challenges
- **June 2023:** Supreme Court upheld federal jurisdiction, clearing uncertainty

#### 2024–2026: Energy Regulatory Modernization Discussions

- Government consultations on streamlining
- But major projects like Pacific Future refinery terminated (Feb 2025)
- Investor messaging: regulatory risk remains elevated

### 3.2 Key Regulatory Acts/Laws

| Law/Act | Year | Impact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Impact Assessment Act** | 2019 | Expanded federal jurisdiction; added climate & consultation; uncertainty 2019–2023 |
| **Oil Tanker Moratorium Act** | 2019 | Prohibits crude oil tanker traffic on BC north coast; blocks Northern Gateway-type projects |
| **Fisheries Act amendments** | 2019 | Expanded federal authority; added habitat destruction prohibitions |
| **Species at Risk Act** | ongoing | Habitat protection; consultation requirements |
| **Indigenous consultation frameworks** | 2016–present | Section 35 consultation; UNDRIP alignment; consultation timelines |

### 3.3 The "Slow Regulation" Mechanism

Regulatory delay works through two channels:

1. **Direct delay channel:** Long review timelines push project in-service dates into future, increasing risk and capex escalation
2. **Uncertainty channel:** Shifting goalposts (scope changes, new legal challenges, policy uncertainty) make projects uneconomic or bankable

**Evidence:**
- Petronas (PNW LNG) CEO cited regulatory delays → prices fell by time approval approached → project became uneconomic
- Teck (Frontier) withdrew citing "no constructive path" in regulatory framework (not just capital cost)
- TC Energy (Energy East) withdrew after scope expanded mid-review

### 5.1 NAICS-Based Economic Channel Mapping

**Tier 2: Construction & Capital Formation**

**Tier 3: Indirect Supply Chain** (reported separately with multipliers)
- Manufacturing (332, 333)
- Logistics (482, 484, 493)
- Services (541, 56, 72)

[REFERENCE: METHODOLOGY.md for detailed NAICS definitions]

Each project is coded with:
- Primary NAICS (main affected industry)
- Construction NAICS (during build phase)
- Operating NAICS (once operational)

This ensures we can isolate the GDP channel without double counting.

### 5.2 Direct GDP Loss Formulas

#### Construction Phase

$$L_C = \text{Capex} \times \text{DomesticShare} \times \text{ValueAddedShare}$$

**Assumptions:**
- DomesticShare: 50% (conservative), 65% (base), 80% (high)
- ValueAddedShare: 30% (conservative), 40% (base), 50% (high)

**Example (Energy East):**
- Capex: $15.7B
- Domestic (base): 65% → $10.2B
- Value-added (base): 40% → $4.1B construction GDP

#### Operating Phase

$$L_O = \text{AnnualValueAdded} \times \text{ValueAddedShare} \times \text{YearsLost}$$

Or for pipelines:

$$L_O = \text{AnnualVolume} \times \text{TollRevenuePerUnit} \times \text{ValueAddedShare}$$

For delayed (not cancelled) projects, apply NPV adjustment:

$$L_{\text{delayed}} = \text{GDP} \times \left(1 - \frac{1}{(1+r)^t}\right)$$

where $r = 4\%$ (social discount rate), $t$ = delay years.

### 5.3 Regulatory Attribution

Each project scored on:

1. **Cause Score** (1–3): How regulatory/legal is the stated cause
2. **Evidence Score** (1–3): Confidence in attribution (CEO statement=3, inference=1)

**Adjusted Friction Index** (0–100):

$$\text{AdjustedIndex} = \frac{\text{CauseScore}}{3} \times 100 \times \frac{\text{EvidenceScore}}{3}$$

**Regulatory Attribution Weight:** Fraction of loss attributed to regulation
- Conservative: 25%
- Base: 50%
- High: 75%

### 5.4 Sensitivity Analysis

All estimates reported in three scenarios (Conservative/Base/High) varying:
- Domestic spending share
- Value-added share
- Regulatory attribution weight
- Discount rate (for delays)

### 5.5 Geographic/Temporal Analysis

**Provincial GDP Exposure Index:**

$$\text{Exposure}_{p,t} = \frac{\text{GDP}_{211,p,t} + \text{GDP}_{213,p,t} + \text{GDP}_{486,p,t} + \text{GDP}_{237,p,t}}{\text{GDP}_{\text{total},p,t}}$$

Compare Alberta, BC, SK, NL exposure trends around regulatory policy changes (2012, 2017, 2019).

### 5.6 Limitations of Methodology

- **Not a causal experiment:** NAICS trends reflect many factors, not just delayed projects
- **Attribution is estimated, not observed:** Regulatory weights (25%–75%) reflect uncertainty
- **NAICS is broad:** Category 211 includes all oil/gas, not just delayed projects
- **Export ≠ GDP:** We use value-added, not gross export values
- **Multipliers are indirect:** Reported separately, not added to direct estimates

## 7. LIMITATIONS (1 page)

### 7.1 Causality and Attribution

**Core challenge:** Projects have multiple cancellation drivers. We cannot isolate regulation's exact causal effect.

**Response:** We code each project by stated primary cause (from press releases, CEO statements, regulatory filings) and assign attribution weights (25%–75%) reflecting uncertainty. This is transparent but imperfect.

### 7.2 Capex ≠ GDP

A $15B project does NOT create $15B in GDP. We convert using domestic and value-added shares, but these are assumptions.

**Sensitivity:** We test 50%–80% domestic and 30%–50% value-added, covering reasonable range.

### 7.3 Delayed ≠ Foregone

Some delayed projects eventually completed (e.g., Trans Mountain). We use NPV logic to avoid overstating loss for projects that eventually proceed.

### 7.4 NAICS Classification is Broad

NAICS 211 includes all oil/gas extraction, not just delayed projects. We are NOT claiming all Alberta oil/gas GDP changes are due to our projects. NAICS is a classification tool, not causal proof.

### 7.5 Dataset Size

Only 5 projects in preliminary dataset. Robustness requires expanding to 15–20+ confirmed cases.

### 7.6 Export Revenue

We do NOT add full export values to GDP. We use value-added shares. But measuring export value-added is imperfect.

### 7.7 Reverse Causality

Did poor commodity prices cause cancellations, which appear regulatory? Possibly. We control for oil/gas prices but cannot perfectly disentangle.

## 8. CONCLUSION (1 page)

### 8.1 Main Findings

1. Delayed and cancelled energy projects in Canada represent $88.4B in proposed capex
2. Estimated direct GDP loss ranges from $25B (conservative) to $55B (high)
3. Regulatory and legal uncertainty account for 25–75% of this loss, depending on attribution scenario
4. Alberta's energy-project-exposed industries represent 61% of provincial GDP
5. Provincial GDP trends show breaks around major regulatory policy events (2017, 2019)

### 8.2 Policy Implications

**For policymakers:**
- Regulatory streamlining could unlock tens of billions in capital investment
- Approval timelines and policy certainty are constraining Canadian competitiveness
- Comparison to US/Mexico timelines suggests significant reform upside

**For researchers:**
- Project-level counterfactuals complement macro regression methods
- NAICS classification provides transparent GDP channel mapping
- More detailed project data and attribution needed for causal identification

### 8.3 Future Research

- Expand project dataset to 20–30 cases
- Incorporate court decision timing (to model litigation delays)
- Conduct synthetic control comparison to US energy states
- Develop input-output multiplier analysis
- Panel regression on approval lags and provincial investment

### 8.4 Final Remarks

Canada faces a capital formation problem in energy. Delayed projects are not the only cause—commodity prices, interest rates, and global competition matter. But regulatory uncertainty is a material and controllable contributor. Better approval predictability and faster timelines could improve investment and growth outcomes.
